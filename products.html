Serengeti National Park

Its name, derived from the Maasai word "siringet," meaning "endless plains," aptly captures its expansive and seemingly boundless beauty. Here, the rhythm of life pulses in harmony with the cycle of the seasons, creating one of the most biodiverse and awe-inspiring ecosystems on Earth.

Mount Kilimanjaro, Africa's tallest peak and the world's highest free-standing mountain, captivates the imagination with its towering presence and mystique. Situated in Tanzania, this majestic stratovolcano rises dramatically from the surrounding plains, its snow-capped summit beckoning adventurers and nature lovers from around the globe. Here are some of the wonders that make Kilimanjaro a truly awe-inspiring destination:

Explore Kilimanjaro National Park: Kilimanjaro National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, encompasses the mountain and its surrounding ecosystems. Gombe National Park

Gombe stream National park covers the area of 35 square kilometres only, this small part of land is among the smallest of Tanzania’s national parks. The park is categorized by the presence of lush forest and steep rich valleys patterned with water streams all year around.
It is also famous for being place for Jane Goodall’s research of chimpanzees which started in the 1960s and is still ongoing to this day, making it the longest running study of its kind in the world.
Despite its small size, Gombe stream national park still offers a good mix of scenarios, rolling grasslands, evergreen and steep slopes covered in semi-deciduous forest. It Borders Lake Tanganyika to the west and the high rift valley escarpment to the east.

BEST TIME TO VISIT GOMBE.
The best time to visit and see the chimpanzees is from late June through October. July to October is considered as the peak season as there is plenty of sunshine and the chimps stay on the lower slopes which makes it even more easy to spot them.

Saadani National Park

Saadani National Park is located in the centre of historic village of Bagamoyo, Pangani tanga, and Zanzibar. The park is bordered by the Indian Ocean on the eastern side while the Wami River forms the southern boundary of the Park.
The park is famous for being the only park in Africa bordering costal region (Indian ocean), where the bush meets the beach. Saadani National park can also be easily accessible from both Dar es Salaam, Tanga and Zanzibar Island.
Saadani National park has an area of 1062 km2 and before it was gazetted as a national park in 2005, Saadani was a game reserve since the year of 1969.
The Saadani National Park is home to abundance of both marine and terrestrial flora and fauna. The vegetation in the park is quite unique and includes mangrove forests around the curving Wami River. The park also has ocean, bunches of palm trees, coral reefs in the Indian Ocean, short and tall-grass savannah and the harsh black cotton plains.

BEST TIME TO VISIT SAADANI NATIONAL PARK.

Fortunately, the Saadani National park can be visited whole year, rainy and the dry season. The dry season in Tanzania starts in June to October. On the other hand, the wet season begins in November, through December, January, February, March, April to May. The less rains are from December to February.
The park experiences a tropical hot weather with the average temperature range of 25-27°C throughout the year. The normal day time temperature, while at night, the temperatures drop to an average of 20 to 24 °C.
So, the peak time to visit Saadani National park is from June to October when the park is dry, wildlife visibility is good as vegetations get thinner and it is sunny.

Mount Kilimanjaro

Mount Kilimanjaro: As Africa's highest peak and the world's tallest freestanding mountain, Kilimanjaro is a bucket-list destination for many climbers. Rising 5,895 meters (19,341 feet) above sea level, Kilimanjaro offers several trekking routes, each with its own challenges and highlights.
The most popular routes include the Marangu, Machame, Lemosho, and Rongai routes, each varying in duration and difficulty. Climbers can traverse diverse landscapes, from lush rainforests to alpine deserts and glaciated slopes, before reaching the iconic Uhuru Peak, the highest point on the mountain. Summiting Kilimanjaro is a challenging but rewarding experience, offering panoramic views of the African plains below.

Kilimanjaro Health Issues

Health Issues you can encounter when climbing Mount Kilimanjaro. This are major health issues you may encounter while taking on the challenge to hike Mount Kilimanjaro

Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS)
Acute Mountain Sickness is also referred to as “Altitude Sickness” as the name indicates the illness is commonly encountered at exceptionally high altitudes, such as the summit area of Mount Kilimanjaro. This is a very common issue about 3 out of 4 hikers will suffer to some extent from the altitude sickness, it is therefore important to familiarize yourself with this condition and preventive measures

Cause of altitude sickness “AMS”, as altitude increases the number of oxygen molecules per breath is reduced. At about 12000 feet (3600m) there is about 40% fewer oxygen molecules per breath so the failure of the body to adapt quickly to the reduced oxygen causes altitude sickness

The symptoms of altitude sickness “AMS” include in the order normally experienced; headaches, nausea, anorexia, exhaustion, lassitude, rapid pulse, insomnia, swelling of the hands and feet and reduced urine output.

What to do if you get altitude sickness “AMS”, the most effective treatment will be by immediately taking the affected person to a lower altitude. Often a drop as little as 500m will be sufficient.

Preventive measures to avoid of altitude sickness “AMS”, Climbers can take precautions to at least minimize the severity of the illness

by maintaining a slow steady pace from day one giving enough time for your body to adapt to the altitude. “pole pole” is a common Swahili phrase translating to slowly, which is now a norm among hikers climbing Mount Kilimanjaro

including an extra day of acclimatization at a high altitude
drinking at least 3-4 liters of water every day.
Preventative medicine is also available and you should consult your physician for specialist advice.

Mount Oldonyo Lengai

Mount Oldoinyo Lengai, towering over the East African Rift Valley in Tanzania, is a geological marvel and a sacred landmark for the Maasai people. Its name translates to "Mountain of God" in the Maasai language, hinting at the reverence it commands among the local communities.

At 2,878 meters (9,442 feet) above sea level, Oldoinyo Lengai is not the tallest peak in Africa, but it stands out for its unique geological composition and cultural significance. What sets it apart is its rare natrocarbonatite lava, which is the only known active volcano in the world to produce such lava. Natrocarbonatite is rich in sodium and potassium carbonates, giving it a distinctive black color when cooled. This lava has a remarkably low viscosity, allowing it to flow more like water than typical lava, and it solidifies into a distinctively brittle, light-colored material.

The mountain's eruptions are not explosive like those of many other volcanoes. Instead, they often feature gentle, effusive flows of the unique natrocarbonatite lava. However, despite its relative quietness, Oldoinyo Lengai poses some hazards due to the extreme heat and the unpredictable nature of volcanic activity.

For the Maasai people, Oldoinyo Lengai holds deep spiritual significance. They believe it to be the abode of their god, Engai, and the place where the earth was created. Maasai elders often lead pilgrimages to the mountain, where they perform rituals and prayers, seeking blessings and guidance. The slopes of the mountain are scattered with sacred sites, where offerings are made to appease the gods and seek protection for their communities and livestock.

Beyond its cultural and geological significance, Oldoinyo Lengai also attracts adventurers and scientists from around the world. Climbing the mountain presents a unique challenge due to the loose, shifting volcanic ash and the steep gradient of its slopes. However, those who undertake the journey are rewarded with breathtaking views of the surrounding Rift Valley and Lake Natron, a soda lake known for its vibrant pink hue due to algae and minerals.

In recent years, efforts have been made to balance the conservation of Oldoinyo Lengai's natural and cultural heritage with tourism and scientific research. Sustainable tourism practices are being promoted to minimize the impact on the fragile ecosystem surrounding the mountain, while ongoing studies help to better understand its volcanic activity and geological features.

Mount Oldoinyo Lengai stands as a testament to the intersection of nature, culture, and spirituality—a place where ancient traditions meet the forces of the Earth in a timeless dance.

Rivers & Lakes

Lake Natron

Lake Natron, located in northern Tanzania, near the border with Kenya, is a remarkable body of water renowned for its surreal and otherworldly appearance. Its name derives from the naturally occurring sodium carbonate compounds present in its waters, giving it a distinctive red or pink hue, especially noticeable during the dry season.

This lake is a salt lake, meaning it has high levels of salt and other minerals. Its unique coloration is due to the presence of cyanobacteria and other microorganisms, which thrive in the highly alkaline environment created by the sodium carbonate. These microorganisms produce pigments that tint the water, resulting in the striking reddish-pink colors that often characterize Lake Natron.

Despite its harsh conditions, Lake Natron is home to a surprising array of life, particularly adapted species of flamingos. The lake is one of the most important breeding grounds for lesser flamingos in East Africa. These birds are attracted to the abundance of microscopic algae and invertebrates that thrive in the salty waters. Their pink plumage complements the lake's color, creating a stunning spectacle against the backdrop of the arid landscape.

Lake Natron stands as a testament to the resilience of life in even the most extreme environments. Its hauntingly beautiful landscapes and diverse wildlife remind us of the intricate interplay between geology, biology, and culture in shaping the world around us.

Lake Victoria

Lake Victoria, nestled in the heart of East Africa, is a majestic water body that captivates with its sheer size, ecological significance, and cultural importance. As the largest lake in Africa and the world's second-largest freshwater lake by surface area, Lake Victoria holds a special place in the region's landscape and collective consciousness.

Spanning approximately 68,800 square kilometers (26,600 square miles) and shared by three countries—Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania—Lake Victoria is a lifeline for millions of people who depend on its waters for sustenance, transportation, and livelihoods.

Serval Wildlife Sanctuary

Our ecotourism lodge is environmentally friendly and working towards a carbon-neutral future. We ensure this by restoring biodiversity within our land through planting endemic species and solely rely on renewable energy sources to generate our electricity. Currently, all the facilities on our land are powered by solar power.

Our sanctuary is over 1000 acres, prepared in phases and in accordance with the needs of our animals 50 acres at a time. Animals are ethically obtained from zoos & injured animals from the wild with the aim of instilling natural behaviors and eventually rehabilitating them into the wild with the assistance of TAWA and TAWIRI. The animals that reside within our wildlife sanctuary are owned by the Tanzanian Government and often brought to us by TAWA as orphans or injured wildlife. We treat and train our animals to prepare them for release into their natural habitat within TANAPA protected parks. Our wildlife sanctuary also doubles as an endangered species reintroduction platform to translocate animals to parts of its natural range where populations have significantly declined or been lost. Aiming to establish a viable population within translocated areas.
